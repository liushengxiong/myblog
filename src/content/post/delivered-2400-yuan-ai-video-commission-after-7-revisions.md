## I Underestimated This Project From the Start

The client sent me a safety education animation script.

![Client requirements script](/assets/images/posts/ai-video-order/image3.png)

It didn't look like much text.

But when it came to actual production, much of the content was highly abstract.

For example:

- Problems reoccurring after corrective actions
- Broken chains of responsibility
- Buck-passing at every level
- Closed-loop rectification and implementation

In the script, each of these was just one sentence.

But I needed to turn these abstract concepts into actual visual scenes, and I couldn't figure out what they should look like no matter how long I thought about it.

Then I came up with an idea — I threw the script directly into AI and let it generate whatever visuals it wanted. Surprisingly, this approach yielded a pleasant surprise. The "broken chain of responsibility" segment in the promotional video was generated by AI in a single attempt. I looked at it and felt the effect was excellent, and the client was very satisfied too.

![The "broken chain of responsibility" scene generated by AI in one shot](/assets/images/posts/ai-video-order/image4.png)

---

## I Discovered for the First Time That AI Generation Isn't the Hardest Part

Many people think the hardest part of AI video production is the software.

It's actually not.

The真正 challenge is understanding what the client wants to express.

I remember there was a segment in the script that read:

The same problem appeared for two consecutive years, recurring after being corrected.

I stared at the script for a long time.

How on earth was I supposed to visualize this?

Later, I could only keep confirming with the client:

What exactly does this scene mean?

What effect needs to be presented?

Can we represent this with charts or text?

Similar questions came up many times throughout the project.

Often, it wasn't that I didn't know how to do it.

It was that I didn't dare make decisions on my own.

Because the client wanted accurate expression, not flashy techniques. Through communication, we finally confirmed that voiceover narration could represent this part.

![Confirming visual expression methods with the client](/assets/images/posts/ai-video-order/image5.png)

## The Most Nerve-Wracking Part Was Waiting for Leadership Review

When the project reached the midpoint.

The client sent me a message:

"The overall effect is fine. Let our leader take a look this afternoon, and then we'll polish the details and it'll be perfect."

![Client's review message](/assets/images/posts/ai-video-order/image6.png)

When I saw this message.

I didn't feel any relief at all.

Instead, I felt even more nervous.

Because I knew.

What really determines whether a project gets approved isn't the contact person.

It's the review process behind them.

The first part, "overall effect is fine," didn't matter — what mattered was the second half: "polish the details."

Anyone who has made AI videos knows that AI currently can't generate content with complete freedom. Many modifications can't be very precise — it's like drawing cards from a gacha game.

So I could only silently pray that the "leadership" wouldn't come up with unreasonable modification requests.

## Even a Single Character Design Could Be Debated Repeatedly

That same evening.

New revision requests started coming in.

One character had a beard.

The leadership felt the image wasn't positive enough.

It needed to be adjusted.

![Request to modify character design](/assets/images/posts/ai-video-order/image7.png)

![Revision feedback messages](/assets/images/posts/ai-video-order/image8.png)

Then there was the voiceover pronunciation.

Subtitle positioning.

Text spacing.

Visual details.

They were all pointed out one by one.

Honestly.

If this were a regular client.

Many people probably wouldn't even notice these details.

But this project made me realize:

Large state-owned enterprises have extremely high standards for content review.

Many seemingly insignificant details.

Were all seriously discussed.

Seriously revised.

It was also my first time learning that in a state-owned enterprise, having a beard could be seen as unkempt — even when the character was supposed to be a villain, someone being reprimanded for mistakes, they still had to maintain the glorious, positive image of the state-owned enterprise.

## Getting Messages at 9 PM Surprised Me

During the project.

One detail left a particularly deep impression on me.

Based on the client's requirements, by the time I finished revising the video that day, it was already past 9 PM.

I originally thought the review process would pause.

But messages kept coming in the enterprise WeChat group.

The client was replying.

The leadership was reviewing.

By 8 AM the next morning, a revision document of over a thousand words was already in my hands.

The entire project kept moving forward.

At that moment, I suddenly realized:

Many people's impression of state-owned enterprises stops at "stable" and "easy."

But after actually working with them, I discovered that many people in these positions work just as diligently.

And they handle work tasks even after hours.

They scrutinized this video meticulously, down to every detail.

## AI Can Improve Efficiency, But It Can't Solve Delivery

This project was ultimately completed successfully.

Looking back and reflecting.

My biggest takeaway wasn't really about how much money I earned.

But rather a deeper understanding of one thing:

AI can improve production efficiency.

But what truly creates value is never the tool itself.

It's:

- Ability to understand requirements
- Project communication skills
- Revision and feedback handling
- Delivery management capabilities

Many people think AI video is just about entering a prompt.

Then clicking generate.

Only after actually taking on projects do you realize:

Generation is just the beginning.

Delivery is the real challenge.

To successfully close the AI monetization loop, beyond mastering AI tools, you need a deep understanding of traffic, sales, and marketing. Only then can you achieve twice the result with half the effort.
