## Before This Trip, My Impression of Hong Kong Was Superficial

Whenever Hong Kong came up in conversation, my mental image was always the same: Victoria Harbour, Tsim Sha Tsui, shopping, milk tea, and dense clusters of skyscrapers.

This time was different. I stayed for a full seven days. I finally had the chance to slow down and experience the city like a local, not just rush through a checklist of tourist spots.

## What Surprised Me Most About Hong Kong Was Its Respect for Time

When I first arrived, my top priority was opening bank accounts. But the first thing that genuinely impressed me? **Free public WiFi.**

I used to dismiss free WiFi as basically useless. But in Hong Kong, public hotspots are everywhere — MTR stations, malls, even street corners. When you need to download banking apps, upload documents, and receive SMS verification codes, that connectivity is a game changer.

As the days went by, I realized this wasn't a fluke. It's a pattern across Hong Kong's public infrastructure.

### The MTR Caught Me Off Guard

I've been to plenty of cities, and what strikes me most about Hong Kong's subway is this: **efficiency.**

At many interchange stations, you step off one train and the opposite platform is right there. No long walks, no endless staircases. You can transfer in under a minute. The signage is so clear that even first-timers rarely get lost.

One detail stuck with me: inside the carriages, the system displays the entire MTR network, not just your current line. You always know exactly where you are in the bigger picture. It's incredibly visitor-friendly.

And here's the kicker — there are no security checkpoints. You just walk in. The first time I experienced that, it felt almost strange.

## On June 13, I Set Myself a Crazy Goal

On my third day in Hong Kong, I decided to attempt something ambitious: **open seven bank accounts in a single day.**

The list:

- HSBC Hong Kong
- Bank of China (Hong Kong)
- Hang Seng Bank
- ZA Bank
- Airstar Bank (formerly Tianxing)
- WeLab Bank
- Ant Bank

When I told friends about this, most reactions were: "Seriously? Is that even possible?"

Turns out, it's not that hard anymore. Many Hong Kong digital banks now offer fully online onboarding. As long as you're physically in Hong Kong and have your ID card, Hong Kong-Macau travel permit, entry record, and a working phone number for SMS, you're basically good to go.

That day, I roamed the city, hopped between free WiFi hotspots, downloaded apps, uploaded documents, did facial recognition, set passwords, and recorded account details. Over and over again. It took several hours. When the final approval came through, I finally exhaled.

## After Opening 7 Cards, I Realized Most People Focus on the Wrong Thing

Many people think the hardest part is getting the account approved. It isn't. **What matters is everything that comes after.**

### 1. Paying for AI Tools

If you use ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini regularly, a Hong Kong bank card has become essential infrastructure. Many international services require a stable payment method that just works.

### 2. Hong Kong E-Wallets

Link your card to WeChat Pay HK and Alipay HK, and daily spending becomes seamless. There are even cashback promotions when you spend back on the mainland. The more you use it, the more you save.

### 3. Cross-Border Money Management

This is the real core value. Most people don't get stuck at account opening — they get stuck on the questions that follow. How do I move money in? How do I get it out? How do I exchange currency? How do I transfer between accounts? These problems are often harder than opening the account itself. A Hong Kong card is a critical step if you're doing any kind of cross-border business. It makes everything that comes after so much smoother.

## Signup Bonuses: The Opportunity Most People Miss

During my research, I noticed a pattern: most people stop caring the moment their account is approved. But Hong Kong banks run generous new-customer promotions all the time. Some offer a few hundred HKD, others over a thousand, and occasionally even more. With a little planning, you can collect these rewards almost effortlessly.

The problem? This information is scattered across forums and often outdated. It took me ages to piece together a clear, current picture.

## If You Can Only Open Three Accounts

If you're short on time and can only manage three, here's my recommendation:

**HSBC Hong Kong**

Highest international recognition. Strong long-term value.

**Bank of China (Hong Kong)**

The central hub for fund transfers. Many scenarios simply require it. In my opinion, this is the most important one. There are plenty of ways to send money to Hong Kong for free, but if you want to bring it back to mainland China without fees, BOCHK to Bank of China mainland is by far the easiest route.

**ZA Bank**

Best overall user experience. This is the one I open most often these days.
